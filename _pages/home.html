~10−10 m · Density functional theory

Electron clouds, by the rules of Kohn–Sham.

Before atoms have positions they have orbitals — solutions to a many-body problem we don't solve exactly, but approximate beautifully with DFT. The same machinery gives me energies, forces, elastic moduli, equations of state — all the ingredients I feed into bigger simulations.

~10−9 m · Molecular dynamics

Atoms find their neighbours.

Drop ten thousand atoms in a box, give them forces, integrate Newton at one femtosecond a step. Squeeze. SiO2 rearranges; silicon grows from four bonds to six; pores collapse irreversibly. I track this with ring statistics, Voronoi cells, and DBSCAN clustering.

~106 m · Inside a rocky planet

Hundreds of gigapascals. Thousands of kelvin.

The minerals from the previous scene now make up most of Earth's mantle. At these conditions, materials science is planetary science: the phase you find changes how seismic waves travel through it, and how mass redistributes itself over billions of years.

~107 m · Earth & planetary science

And out comes the planet.

I image Earth's interior with ambient-noise tomography, invert it with full-waveform inversion, and benchmark parts of that inversion on a D-Wave Advantage quantum annealer. Same problem, four scales — qubit to continent.

~1011 m · One star, a few rocky worlds

A pale blue dot, in context.

Earth is one of several planets around an unremarkable star. The same equations of state I study at the atomic scale govern super-Earths and icy moons light-years from here — which is why high-pressure mineral physics is also planetary science.

~1021 m · One galaxy, ~1011 stars

Zoom out a bit more.

A hundred billion suns in a spiral, most of them with planets. Most of those planets are rocky. Most of that rock is silicate. The little simulation cell from a few scenes ago is a sample of the universe's most abundant solid.
