Quantum computing · annealing · gate-based · hybrid

Inverting seismic waves
with qubits.

Full-waveform inversion is a high-dimensional, non-convex optimization — exactly the kind of problem people want to throw at a quantum computer. I work across three flavors: annealing on the D-Wave Advantage (reformulating subproblems as QUBO), gate-based quantum hardware (variational algorithms on IBM-style devices), and hybrid quantum–classical methods that hand specific kernels to the quantum solver while the rest stays on classical HPC.

~10−10 m · Density functional theory

Electron clouds.

Before atoms have positions they have orbitals — solutions to a many-body problem we don't solve exactly, but approximate beautifully with DFT, running on national HPC clusters. The same machinery gives me energies, forces, elastic moduli, equations of state — all the ingredients I feed into bigger simulations.

~10−9 m · Molecular dynamics

Atoms find their neighbours.

Drop ten thousand atoms in a box, give them forces, integrate Newton at one femtosecond a step. Squeeze. SiO2 rearranges; silicon grows from four bonds to six; pores collapse irreversibly. I run these on HPC and pull structure out with ring statistics, Voronoi cells, DBSCAN clustering — and increasingly with deep learning models that learn the order parameters the hand-crafted descriptors miss.

~106 m · Inside a rocky planet

Hundreds of gigapascals. Thousands of kelvin.

The minerals from the previous scene now make up most of Earth's mantle. At these conditions, materials science is planetary science: the phase you find changes how seismic waves travel through it, and how mass redistributes itself over billions of years.

~107 m · Earth & planetary science

And out comes the planet.

I image Earth's interior with ambient-noise tomography and invert it with full-waveform inversion — the same inversion problem we threw at the quantum annealer at the top of this page, just from the planetary side instead of the qubit side.

~1011 m · One star, a few rocky worlds

A pale blue dot, in context.

Earth is one of several planets around an unremarkable star. The same equations of state I study at the atomic scale govern super-Earths and icy moons light-years from here — which is why high-pressure mineral physics is also planetary science.

~1021 m · One galaxy, ~1011 stars

Zoom out a bit more.

A hundred billion suns in a spiral, most of them with planets. Most of those planets are rocky. Most of that rock is silicate. The little simulation cell from a few scenes ago is a sample of the universe's most abundant solid.
